\begin{abstract}
Sensitive text corpora are increasingly prepared for retrieval, evaluation, sharing, and model adaptation by transforming one document at a time.
This document-local workflow removes obvious direct identifiers, but it can leave a corpus-level failure mode: records with no names, emails, or account numbers may still be linkable through repeated quasi-identifiers such as role, region, institution type, family context, health context, financial context, schedules, and rare events.
We introduce \bench, a fully synthetic benchmark with 120 personas, 480 documents, risk tiers, utility labels, decoy auxiliary profiles, and RAG-style exposure tasks.
We show that common document-local transformations remain linkable: direct redaction leaves local auxiliary matching at 0.71 top-1, a Presidio-style PII baseline remains at 0.59, and a GPT-5.5 auxiliary attacker matches direct-redacted and document-local corpora almost perfectly on a 48-person T2/T3 stress audit.
We propose \method, a simple corpus-aware generalization heuristic that estimates cross-document uniqueness and generalizes high-risk quasi-identifiers until a target anonymity set is reached.
At target $k=5$, \method{} reduces local auxiliary top-1 to 0.04 while preserving issue classification and retrieval Recall@5 at 1.00; aggressive redaction reaches similar local privacy but drops issue accuracy to 0.35 and retrieval Recall@5 to 0.31.
These synthetic results suggest that LLM-ready data transformations should be evaluated for corpus-level linkage resistance, not only document-local PII removal.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Sensitive data is often most useful when it is not isolated.
A hospital may have clinical messages, billing notes, scheduling records, and accommodation requests about the same patient.
A legal-aid organization may have intake notes, benefits appeals, landlord disputes, and follow-up emails about the same client.
A company may have support tickets, HR notes, finance requests, and internal chat snippets about the same employee or customer.
Modern foundation-model workflows---retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), evaluation-set construction, model adaptation, analytics, and data sharing---therefore often operate over \emph{multi-document} corpora rather than independent documents \citep{lewis2020rag,zeng2024ragprivacy,jiang2024ragthief}.

The dominant operational response is document-local de-identification: remove names, emails, phone numbers, mailing addresses, account numbers, and other spans that look identifying within one record.
That response is necessary, but it misses the central lesson of quasi-identifier privacy: combinations of ordinary attributes can distinguish people even when direct identifiers are gone \citep{sweeney2002,narayanan2008,cohen2022attacks}.
In text, this problem is harder because the identifiers are semantic rather than tabular.
A region, occupation, institution type, care schedule, family constraint, legal context, and local event may each look harmless in isolation, yet together they can form a stable signature across documents.
Pseudonymization can make the problem worse: a stable pseudonym helps preserve continuity for benign users, but it is also a cross-document linkage handle.

LLM-ready data makes this failure mode more urgent.
Recent work shows that language models can infer personal attributes from text even when the text does not contain explicit identifiers \citep{staab2024beyond}; text anonymization benchmarks increasingly emphasize residual re-identification risk rather than span recall alone \citep{pilan2022tab,krco2026ratbench}; and RAG privacy work shows that retrieval databases can leak private records through retrieval and generation channels \citep{zeng2024ragprivacy,jiang2024ragthief}.
This paper studies a focused question: \emph{after document-local anonymization or pseudonymization, can an attacker still link multiple transformed documents to the same person and match them to auxiliary background profiles?}
We intentionally avoid real-person data and real re-identification attempts.
Instead, every persona, document, decoy, and utility label is generated synthetically, giving us safe ground truth for controlled privacy auditing.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
    \setlength{\parsep}{0pt}
    \setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark.} \bench{} is a synthetic cross-document privacy benchmark with 120 personas, 480 documents, risk tiers, utility labels, document templates, and decoy auxiliary profiles.
    \item \textbf{Audit protocol.} We evaluate transformations with document linkage, auxiliary-profile matching, quasi-identifier recovery, RAG-style exposure, GPT-5.5 stress audits, and utility retention.
    \item \textbf{Finding.} Direct redaction, Presidio-style PII removal, document-local anonymization, and stable pseudonyms all leave strong residual linkage risk.
    \item \textbf{Defense.} \method{} is a simple corpus-aware generalization heuristic that improves the synthetic privacy--utility tradeoff over blanket aggressive redaction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Threat Model and Benchmark}

\paragraph{Setting.}
A data holder has a sensitive text corpus with multiple records per person, household, customer, patient, client, or employee.
The corpus is transformed before it is indexed for RAG, shared internally, released for evaluation, or used in model-adaptation workflows.
The holder may know which records belong to the same account, case, patient, or synthetic persona; our method uses this grouping only to estimate repeated quasi-identifier risk.

\paragraph{Attacker.}
The attacker sees the transformed corpus.
For the auxiliary-profile task, the attacker also receives a small candidate set containing one true synthetic auxiliary profile and decoys.
This simulates background knowledge such as a directory, leaked metadata table, public biographical context, or another internal database.
The attacker does not receive original direct identifiers, and candidate labels are neutralized in API audits to avoid leaking synthetic persona IDs.

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
\bench{} contains 120 synthetic personas and 480 documents.
Each persona has direct identifiers, utility labels, and quasi-identifiers: region, city, occupation, employer type, education, family structure, medical context, financial context, legal context, schedule pattern, community affiliation, and rare event.
Each persona has four short service records spanning healthcare, legal, financial support, and HR.
Personas are assigned to three risk tiers: T1 contains mostly generic context, T2 contains moderately specific context, and T3 contains high-linkage combinations.
For each persona, we build a ten-candidate auxiliary set with one true profile and nine decoys that share partial attributes.
This design lets us measure whether a transformation protects against both document linkage and background-knowledge matching while retaining task utility.

\section{Transformations and \method{}}

We compare common document-local transformations against a corpus-aware alternative.
C1 directly redacts names, email addresses, phone numbers, mailing addresses, and account IDs.
C1b applies a Presidio-style PII analyzer plus local regex cleanup.
C2 uses stable pseudonyms across the corpus, while C3 resets pseudonyms per document.
C4 is a document-local anonymization proxy that generalizes selected local spans without seeing neighboring documents.
C5 is \method{}.
C6 is aggressive redaction, suppressing most quasi-identifiers and some task issue phrases.
We also run GPT-5.5 document-local anonymization as an API stress baseline on a synthetic subset.

\paragraph{Key idea.}
Document-local anonymizers ask, ``Is this span identifying in this document?''
\method{} asks, ``Does this repeated detail make the person distinguishable in the corpus?''
For each quasi-identifier field, \method{} defines a three-level ladder:
\[
\text{exact value} \rightarrow \text{semantic category} \rightarrow \text{generic suppression}.
\]
For example, a city becomes a region and then ``a broad location''; an occupation becomes a job family and then ``general worker''; a rare event becomes a local-event category and then ``a local event.''

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
For each persona group, \method{} starts from direct redaction and computes the current estimated anonymity set: the number of personas sharing the same generalized quasi-identifier representation.
If this estimate is below the target $k$, the method chooses another field to generalize.
The field score balances value rarity, a specificity prior, and an approximate utility cost:
\[
s(f) =
\frac{\mathrm{rarity}(f) + \mathrm{specificity}(f) + 1}
{\max(\mathrm{utility\_cost}(f), 0.1)}.
\]
Fields with rarer values, higher specificity, and lower expected utility cost are generalized first.
The process stops when the estimated anonymity set reaches target $k$ or all fields have been generalized or suppressed.
This is a heuristic risk-budgeting procedure, not a formal privacy guarantee: its estimate depends on the corpus, schema, grouping assumptions, and attacker knowledge.

\section{Attacks and Metrics}

\paragraph{Linkage and auxiliary matching.}
We evaluate same-person pairwise linkage, fixed-$K$ clustering, and auxiliary-profile matching.
The main local auxiliary attacker uses word TF-IDF; robustness checks use character TF-IDF, hybrid TF-IDF, and a field-weighted matcher that explicitly scans exact and generalized schema fields.
We report top-1 and top-3 auxiliary accuracy and mean reciprocal rank; lower is better for privacy.

\paragraph{Quasi-identifier recovery.}
Auxiliary matching is an end-to-end privacy signal, but it does not explain what leaked.
We therefore scan transformed documents for exact and coarse recovery of schema fields such as city, role, institution type, family context, medical context, legal context, schedule, affiliation, and rare event.

\paragraph{RAG exposure.}
We issue profile-like queries against each transformed corpus and measure whether target-persona documents appear in the top retrieved results.
We also run a small GPT-5.5 RAG-generation audit in which retrieved T3 contexts are passed to an instruction-following model to test whether it can infer that the documents describe the same synthetic person.

\paragraph{Utility.}
Utility is measured with issue classification, task-oriented retrieval Recall@5, and lightweight fact preservation.
These metrics test whether service-triage information survives transformation; they are not a complete downstream deployment evaluation.

\paragraph{Document-local transformations remain linkable.}
Direct redaction preserves the quasi-identifier signature: the local auxiliary attacker matches the correct profile as often as on the original corpus.
The Presidio-style baseline removes more obvious spans, but still leaves high residual matching.
The document-local anonymization proxy and GPT-5.5 document-local anonymizer show the same weakness: because each document is transformed independently, repeated role, location, schedule, institution, and rare-event patterns remain available across the corpus.
Stable pseudonyms create a different failure mode: the pseudonym itself becomes a linkage handle, making pairwise linkage nearly trivial.

\paragraph{\method{} improves the privacy--utility frontier.}
\method{} sharply reduces local auxiliary matching and exact quasi-identifier recovery while preserving issue classification and retrieval utility.
Aggressive redaction reaches a similar local privacy level, but it removes task-relevant issue information and hurts retrieval.
This contrast is the main practical lesson: for LLM-ready corpora, the best transformation is not always the most destructive one; it is the one that removes the high-linkage fields while retaining task information.

\paragraph{The risk is corpus-level, not just span-level.}
Risk-tier analysis confirms the benchmark behaves as intended: direct redaction is mostly adequate on generic T1 personas, but fails on T2 and T3 personas where repeated quasi-identifiers are more specific.
Ablations show that role/institution and location are the largest single drivers, while family, health, schedule, and rare-event fields become important in combination or under a field-aware attacker.
A corpus-awareness ablation further supports the method design: using true corpus co-occurrence statistics reaches the target anonymity coverage, while shuffled co-occurrence planning and global one-level generalization either miss the target or leave strong field-aware matching.

\paragraph{GPT-5.5 and RAG audits corroborate the local story.}
The GPT-5.5 auxiliary audit is intentionally a time-stamped stress test rather than the main reproducible evidence, but it strengthens the conclusion.
On the T2/T3 subset, the model almost perfectly matches direct-redacted, Presidio-redacted, and document-local corpora; \method{} remains substantially less matchable and much more uncertain, though not risk-free.
A GPT-5.5 evidence-extraction audit explains why: successful direct-redaction matches cite location and role, while \method{} residual matches rarely cite high-specificity fields and are usually marked uncertain.
The RAG diagnostics tell the same story from a retrieval perspective: exact and generated profile-like queries easily retrieve high-linkage direct/document-local records, whereas \method{} greatly reduces exact field recovery in retrieved contexts.

\paragraph{Robustness.}
The qualitative ordering holds across three synthetic corpus seeds, a noisy-style rerendering of the documents, auxiliary candidate sets of size 10/20/50, and word/character/hybrid/field-weighted local attackers.
The most important caveat is that the field-weighted attacker still finds residual coarse-context signal under \method{}.
This is expected: \method{} generalizes high-risk fields; it does not erase all semantics.

\section{Related Work}

Classic quasi-identifier privacy work motivates our setting.
$k$-anonymity introduced generalization and suppression as ways to reduce record uniqueness \citep{sweeney2002}, while later work showed that sparse or high-dimensional records can be de-anonymized with limited auxiliary knowledge \citep{narayanan2008}.
Subsequent attacks and critiques show that quasi-identifier de-identification can fail even when many attributes are treated as potential identifiers \citep{cohen2022attacks}.
Related privacy models such as $\ell$-diversity and $t$-closeness address sensitive-attribute homogeneity and distributional leakage, but they remain difficult to apply directly to free text \citep{machanavajjhala2007ldiversity,li2007tcloseness}.

Text anonymization benchmarks increasingly move beyond named-entity deletion.
TAB provides legal-text annotations and privacy-oriented evaluation for text anonymization \citep{pilan2022tab}.
RAT-Bench explicitly evaluates residual re-identification risk from direct and indirect identifiers in text, which is closely aligned with our emphasis on quasi-identifiers \citep{krco2026ratbench}.
Surveys of anonymization techniques and metrics emphasize that privacy, utility, legal requirements, and human expectations are often measured inconsistently \citep{deusser2025textanon,ren2025metrics}.
Tools such as Presidio and OpenAI Privacy Filter are useful PII-detection components, but PII detection alone is not a complete anonymization guarantee \citep{presidio,openai2026privacyfilter}.

LLM privacy work motivates stronger attackers.
Language models can infer private attributes from text beyond direct memorization \citep{staab2024beyond}, and large models can reveal memorized training data under extraction attacks \citep{carlini2021extracting,nasr2023scalable}.
RAG systems add a retrieval database that can itself leak sensitive records \citep{zeng2024ragprivacy,jiang2024ragthief}.
Our contribution is complementary: we focus on cross-document linkage after document-level transformations, where repeated corpus-level quasi-identifiers can survive otherwise reasonable LLM-ready anonymization workflows.

\section{Limitations and Ethics}

The benchmark is synthetic and template-controlled.
This gives safe ground truth and reproducibility, but limits external validity.
Real sensitive corpora may contain richer writing styles, temporal dependencies, institutional terminology, missing fields, contradictions, OCR errors, and adversary knowledge.
The GPT-5.5 audits are cached, time-stamped synthetic subset stress tests; deterministic local sweeps remain the main reproducible evidence.
\method{} is a heuristic generalization method, not a formal privacy guarantee.
Utility is measured with issue labels, retrieval, and lightweight fact checks rather than full downstream deployments.

The benchmark is intended for defensive auditing.
We do not collect, process, or attempt to re-identify real people.
All examples are synthetic and generated from a controlled schema.
Any extension to real data would require authorization, data minimization, access controls, logging, and ethical review.
Practitioners should not treat success on this benchmark as proof that a real corpus is safe to release, index, or train on.

\section{Conclusion}

Document-local PII removal is not enough for LLM-ready sensitive corpora.
In a multi-document setting, repeated quasi-identifiers can support linkage, auxiliary-profile matching, and profile-query retrieval even after direct identifiers are removed.
Our synthetic benchmark suggests that privacy evaluation should include corpus-level linkage resistance, not only span-level PII recall.
It also suggests that simple corpus-aware generalization can improve the privacy--utility frontier relative to both document-local anonymization and blanket redaction.
The broader lesson is that data transformation for foundation models should ask what a capable attacker can still infer across the corpus, not only what identifying spans were removed from each document.
